# Talk Memorizer — User Guide

A skill that helps you memorize, practice, and master your conference talks using active recall quizzing, visual mnemonics, study guides, and interactive rehearsal. Designed for speakers preparing multiple talks on a deadline.

## Getting Started

### Install the Skill

Save the `talk-memorizer.skill` file using the install button. Once installed, the skill activates automatically whenever you mention practicing, memorizing, or rehearsing a talk.

### Load a Talk

Provide two files for each talk:

- A **speaker script** (.md) — your full script with `## [SLIDE N — Title]` markers, stage directions in italics, and timing checkpoints
- A **slide deck** (.pptx) — the matching presentation

Just say something like: *"Here's my talk on AI security — help me start learning it"* and attach both files. The skill will parse the script, extract all the key data (stats, transitions, audience interactions, timing), and ask what you'd like to do first.

You can load all 4 of your talks this way. The skill tracks progress across all of them and suggests which one needs the most attention.

## The Five Modes

You can use any mode at any time. Just ask for what you want, or let the skill suggest based on your progress.

### 1. Study Guide Generation

**Say**: *"Give me a study guide"*, *"cheat sheet"*, *"key points"*, *"something to review on my commute"*

Generates files you can read on your phone or print out:

- **Structure Map** — The arc of your talk with 2-3 word memory anchors per slide
- **Stats & Facts Drill Sheet** — Every number, date, name, and citation organized by section
- **Timing Guide** — Checkpoints, which sections to compress if running long, anchor sections you shouldn't cut
- **Transition Map** — The bridge sentences between every section
- **Audience Interaction Cheat Sheet** — Every poll, exercise, and pause with setup instructions

### 2. Quiz & Active Recall

**Say**: *"Quiz me"*, *"test me"*, *"drill me on stats"*, *"what comes after the RAG walkthrough?"*

Interactive back-and-forth quizzing. The skill asks one question at a time, waits for your answer, then gives feedback. Question types include flow/sequence, stats/details, transitions, audience interactions, timing, fill-in-the-blank, and memory palace recall.

The quizzing adapts to your weak areas. After a round of 5-8 questions, you get a summary of what you're strong on and what needs more work, with an offer to do a focused round on your gaps.

You can ask for a focused quiz: *"Just quiz me on the numbers"* or *"Test me on transitions between sections."*

### 3. Interactive Rehearsal

**Say**: *"Let me practice the opening"*, *"I want to rehearse Layer 3"*, *"cold open practice"*

You type out what you'd say from memory, and the skill gives detailed feedback comparing your delivery against the actual script. It checks for key points covered, specific details missed (names, dates, stats), audience interactions you skipped, transition quality, and approximate timing.

Rehearsal sub-modes:

- **Section Rehearsal** — Practice any section and get line-by-line feedback
- **Transition Drill** — Given two adjacent slide titles, practice bridging between them
- **Cold Open** — Deliver the full opening from memory
- **Recovery Drill** — "You just blanked somewhere in Layer 3. How do you find your place?" Tests your structural knowledge and ability to recover
- **Timing Rehearsal** — Tracks your word count against the target pace

### 4. Dashboard & Progress

**Say**: *"How am I doing?"*, *"dashboard"*, *"which talk needs the most work?"*, *"progress"*

Shows the current state across all your loaded talks: which modes you've used, quiz accuracy by category, areas flagged for review, and a recommendation for your next practice session.

### 5. Visual Mnemonics & Memory Palace

**Say**: *"Memory palace"*, *"make it visual"*, *"help me remember with images"*, *"weird visuals"*

This is the fun one. It generates spatial and visual memory aids that tap into how your brain actually remembers things — through bizarre, funny, exaggerated imagery rather than abstract concepts.

What it creates:

- **Memory Palace** — Maps your entire talk to rooms in a house. Each room has 2-4 vivid, absurd mental images encoding the key concepts. You "walk through" the house in your mind during delivery, and each room triggers recall of that section.
- **Number-Image Associations** — Every stat paired with a ridiculous image (97 dalmatians storming an unguarded building, a pizza with a third missing for 33%, etc.)
- **Concept Cartoons** — Single-panel cartoon descriptions for key principles. You read them, picture them, and they stick.
- **Acronyms & Mnemonics** — Memory phrases for ordered lists and sequences in your talk
- **Story Anchors** — Exaggerated mental images for each real-world example and incident

## Suggested Workflow

### Two Weeks Before the Conference

**Week 1 — Build the Foundation**

- Day 1-2: Load all 4 talks. Generate study guides for each.
- Day 3-4: Generate memory palaces. Read through them and start visualizing.
- Day 5-7: Daily 10-minute quiz sessions during commutes. Focus on structure and stats.

**Week 2 — Deepen and Rehearse**

- Day 8-9: Rehearse the opening and closing of each talk from memory. Get feedback.
- Day 10-11: Quiz sessions focused on your weak areas (the skill will tell you what they are).
- Day 12-13: Full section rehearsals. Practice transitions between sections.
- Day 14: Recovery drills — practice finding your place after blanking.

### Day of the Talk

- Do a quick memory palace walkthrough (5 minutes, eyes closed, walk through the rooms)
- Glance at the one-page cheat sheet
- Trust that you know the material

## Tips

- **Short sessions beat long ones.** 10 minutes of focused quizzing is worth more than 45 minutes of passive review.
- **Mix the modes.** Don't just read study guides — quiz yourself, rehearse out loud, walk the memory palace. Interleaving different types of practice strengthens memory.
- **The memory palace works best if you really visualize it.** Spend a minute with eyes closed picturing each room. The weirder and more vivid the image, the better it sticks.
- **Don't aim for word-perfect.** The skill evaluates whether you hit the key concepts, not whether you matched the script verbatim. Owning the ideas matters more than reciting sentences.
- **Use the recovery drill.** Speakers fear blanking more than anything. Practicing recovery builds the confidence that even if you lose your place, you can find it again in seconds.
